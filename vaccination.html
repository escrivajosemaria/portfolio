Vaccination & Disease Prevention

Vaccination and immunization are among the most effective public health interventions ever developed. Vaccines work by training the immune system to recognize and fight diseases before they cause serious illness or death. Today, vaccines exist for more than 30 life-threatening diseases, including diphtheria, tetanus, pertussis, measles, polio, hepatitis B, yellow fever, meningitis, and human papillomavirus (HPV), a virus that causes cervical cancer and other cancers, providing protection across the lifespan from infancy through adulthood. Immunization currently prevents an estimated 3.5 to 5 million deaths every year and has saved at least 154 million lives over the past 50 years, the majority of them infants who might not otherwise have survived childhood without vaccination World Health Organization.

Despite these monumental successes, global immunization coverage remains uneven. In 2024, around 14.3 million children worldwide did not receive even a single basic vaccine, commonly referred to as zero-dose children, highlighting persistent access and equity gaps World Health Organization. Routine coverage for key vaccines such as diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis (DTP3) hovered around 85 percent globally, while coverage for yellow fever in at-risk countries was only about 52 percent, far below recommended targets World Health Organization. Measles remains particularly sensitive to even small drops in coverage, and while widespread immunization over recent decades has reduced cases dramatically, immunity gaps continue to fuel periodic outbreaks. Continued investment and strategic action are essential to achieve the goals of the Immunization Agenda 2030, which calls for expanded vaccine access and stronger immunization systems worldwide World Health Organization.

What I Do

In my work on immunization and disease prevention initiatives, I focus on strengthening vaccine delivery across diverse health system contexts. My approach combines technical planning, data-informed decision-making, and community engagement to improve coverage, reduce preventable disease risk, and ensure immunization services reach underserved populations.

  • Support to routine immunization programs: Strengthening planning, delivery, and integration of routine immunization services to improve consistency, equity, and system performance across facilities and communities.
  • Vaccination coverage surveys and assessments: Supporting the design and use of coverage assessments to identify immunization gaps, understand missed populations, and inform targeted, data-driven interventions.
  • Disease prevention strategy development: Contributing to the development of evidence-informed prevention strategies that align immunization efforts with broader public health priorities and epidemiological trends.
  • Community engagement and demand support: Promoting approaches that build trust, address vaccine hesitancy, and strengthen community participation to improve uptake and sustained use of immunization services.
  • Coordination with health system stakeholders: Working with government bodies, partners, and community structures to support aligned planning, efficient implementation, and sustainable disease prevention outcomes.

Core Focus Areas

Routine Immunization

Supporting the planning, coordination, and delivery of routine vaccination services within primary health care systems to ensure continuity, equity, and quality of care. Strong routine immunization systems are widely recognized as the foundation of disease prevention, with the World Health Organization emphasizing that routine immunization is the most effective and equitable way to protect populations from vaccine-preventable diseases.

Coverage Surveys & Monitoring

Designing and supporting vaccination coverage assessments and monitoring systems to identify underserved populations and geographic gaps. Reliable data is essential for informed decision-making and continuous improvement, as strong monitoring helps guide planning, target resources effectively, and strengthen accountability across immunization programs. World Health Organization

Disease Prevention Strategies

Contributing to the development and implementation of disease prevention strategies that reduce the burden of vaccine-preventable diseases. These strategies are informed by epidemiological evidence and public health priorities, reflecting the long-standing principle articulated by Dr. Anthony Fauci that prevention remains one of the most powerful tools in protecting population health.

Community Engagement & Demand

Working with communities, local leaders, and frontline actors to strengthen trust in vaccination services and improve access and uptake. Community engagement is a critical determinant of immunization success, with UNICEF highlighting the importance of listening to communities, addressing concerns, and ensuring services are culturally appropriate and responsive to local needs.
